# Cover Letter

Dear Editor,

We submit the manuscript entitled "A Receiver-Flux-Aware Reproducible Layout--Aiming Benchmark for the Dunhuang 100 MW Solar Tower" for consideration as a research article in *Solar Energy*.

This manuscript addresses a practical benchmark question: how can a public commercial-scale solar-tower case be used to test heliostat layout variants while keeping optical efficiency, geometry realism, public-terrain screening, aiming behavior, and receiver-flux risk separate? It does not rely on mirror deletion or a simple cost-reduced comparison. It preserves the available 11,915-heliostat coordinate pool, anchors the field to public Dunhuang plant parameters, applies reproducible terrain-screened sector/petal deformations, checks representative layouts against same-condition low-complexity controls with PySAM/SolarPILOT under common default aiming, and adds reduced PySolTrace direct-ray audits with verified reflector-to-receiver stage ordering. The revision also adds a stronger same-anchor pressure test with literature-inspired pattern-free, slider-like, terrain-aware, and hybrid approximation families. These are not presented as complete reproductions of named external optimizers; they are used to test whether stronger local baselines can explain the queue. A fast annual proxy now interpolates the SolarPILOT optical-efficiency tables over the 8760-hour public weather file, scales the public weather to the reported corrected TMY DNI, and checks the baseline proxy against the 351.6 GWh public design-generation reference. This addition strengthens the plant-scale sanity check while keeping bankable annual yield and annual custom aiming outside the claims. A new direct baseline-control audit compares the strongest simple controls and TS-FPDA representatives under identical sampled ray-tracing settings, and a leave-one-day-out strategy-selection audit selects the aiming strategy on eight representative days before testing it on the held-out day. These additions let the manuscript state where simple radial, wave, hybrid, or slider-like effects are sufficient, where full-matrix strategy selection is optimistic, and where a role-based candidate queue remains useful.

The central result is intentionally not a single over-optimized layout. Instead, the work shows that optical gain, default SolarPILOT receiver flux, proxy aiming, baseline controls, same-anchor strong baselines, and reduced direct ray tracing do not select one universal candidate or aiming phase. In particular, the optical-upper candidate improves mean SolarPILOT optical efficiency but increases peak-to-active-mean receiver-flux concentration. Simple radial and wave controls explain part of the receiver-risk response, and the stronger hybrid/slider-like approximation rows show that local geometric effects must remain in the comparison set. The reduced direct checks nevertheless preserve a useful receiver-risk candidate queue when interpreted at the role level. The V12 bootstrap confidence audit, the direct baseline-control audit, and the leave-one-day-out hold-out audit show that only the strongest rows should be described as bounded directional evidence rather than as final aiming prescriptions. This diagnostic result is the main value of the paper: it turns the Dunhuang case into a reproducible layout-and-flux-risk numerical benchmark rather than a claimed final plant redesign.

The manuscript should be of interest to readers working on concentrating solar power, heliostat-field optimization, solar-tower receiver-flux modeling, aiming strategies, and reproducible benchmark design. It is aligned with *Solar Energy* because it combines solar-tower layout methodology, optical-performance numerical verification, receiver-flux analysis, and data/code reproducibility while clearly stating the domain of validity and the remaining verification needs.

All authors have approved the manuscript. The authors declare no competing interests. The manuscript is original, is not under consideration elsewhere, and will be accompanied by the layout files, scripts, figures, numerical-checking tables, and checksum manifest needed to reproduce the reported results. The manuscript-facing GitHub package is versioned as `v0.1.10`; after Zenodo deposition, the final DOI-bearing submission will cite the corresponding Zenodo DOI for that package.
