## 2026-06-22 live-check conclusion

After a fresh XR Scholar and publisher-page check, the recommendation is still to submit first to *Solar Energy*, with a realistic risk label: high but defensible. The current manuscript is not a generic optimization note and is not a former *Scientific Data* descriptor anymore; it is now a plant-anchored heliostat layout--aiming benchmark and numerical-checking workflow with SolarPILOT, reduced PySolTrace, receiver-flux-risk, annual-proxy, strong-baseline, and reproducibility evidence. That is exactly the readership that *Solar Energy* can understand. The paper should not be sold as a state-of-the-art commercial field redesign, a bankable annual-yield study, or a receiver thermal-safety certification.

The no-APC preference is also compatible with *Solar Energy*: the Elsevier journal page lists an open-access APC, but also lists a subscription route with no publication fee charged to authors. At submission, the authors should choose the subscription publication route unless the institution explicitly covers open access.

The fallback ladder is: *Solar Energy* first; *Sustainable Energy Technologies and Assessments* if the story is reframed as a sustainable-energy technology assessment and reproducible decision workflow; *International Journal of Green Energy* if the priority becomes a more conservative SCI route with no mandatory APC. ASME *Journal of Solar Energy Engineering* is a solar-engineering fallback but falls below the Q2/Q3 target in the current XR Scholar signal. *Renewable Energy* and *Applied Thermal Engineering* should not be treated as easier alternatives: their metrics and validation expectations are at least as demanding, and their scope is less directly aligned with the current heliostat benchmark story.

## 2026-06-22 evidence refresh for author decision

The decision should be made as a three-level route rather than a single yes/no on *Solar Energy*:

- **Route A, recommended first attempt:** submit to *Solar Energy* with conservative claims. This is the best match for a CSP/heliostat/receiver-flux paper, and its subscription route satisfies the no-mandatory-APC requirement. The risk is reviewer strictness, not scope mismatch.
- **Route B, prepared fallback:** submit to *Sustainable Energy Technologies and Assessments* if the authors want to reduce the specialist-CSP burden. This needs a light reframing from "solar-tower field benchmark" to "sustainable-energy technology assessment and reproducible screening workflow."
- **Route C, acceptance-oriented SCI fallback:** submit to *International Journal of Green Energy* if the priority becomes SCI stability and no APC. It is less prestigious and less CSP-specific, but its publisher page explicitly states no APC when authors do not choose open access.

This paper should not be downgraded just because *Solar Energy* is difficult. The current 40-page package is strongest when judged by a solar-engineering audience that understands why plant anchoring, full-field preservation, flux screening, and reproducibility matter. A too-general journal may ask for extra techno-economic or policy material that is less natural for this manuscript.

## Executive recommendation

The best first target remains *Solar Energy*. It is demanding, but it is still the most coherent first submission target for the current manuscript because the paper is about a plant-anchored heliostat-field layout--aiming benchmark, SolarPILOT/PySolTrace verification, receiver-flux risk, and solar-tower engineering practice. The manuscript should be submitted as a conservative benchmark and screening-evidence paper, not as a certified commercial plant redesign or state-of-the-art optimizer.

The best prepared fallback is *Sustainable Energy Technologies and Assessments* if the authors want a broader energy-technology-assessment readership. The safer acceptance-oriented SCI fallback is *International Journal of Green Energy*, especially if the priority becomes Q3/SCI stability and a no-mandatory-APC publishing route.

## Journals not recommended as the first target

- *Renewable Energy*: ranking and metrics are stronger than *Solar Energy*, not safer. XR Scholar lists Engineering 1 区 Top and ENERGY & FUELS 2 区; Elsevier reports higher impact metrics. For the current manuscript it would likely require stronger annual techno-economic, receiver-thermal, or cross-code validation.
- *Applied Thermal Engineering*: attractive metrics, but the manuscript is not primarily about receiver heat transfer or thermal-stress modeling.
- *Energy Conversion and Management: X*: strong metrics, but it is open-access/mirror-journal oriented and conflicts with the no-mandatory-APC preference.
- *Journal of Renewable and Sustainable Energy*: viable but too low in XR Scholar for the user's stated Q2/Q3 target.
- MDPI-style OA venues such as *Energies*: easier administratively but poor fit with the no-APC preference and weaker prestige strategy.

## Practical submission decision

My recommendation is to prepare the current package for *Solar Energy* first, with *Sustainable Energy Technologies and Assessments* as the ready fallback. ## Can the current paper reach Solar Energy?

Yes, but only if submitted with the current conservative framing. The realistic acceptance path is not "we invented the best heliostat optimizer"; it is "we provide a reproducible Dunhuang full-field layout--aiming benchmark with receiver-flux-aware screening and direct-promotion evidence." The paper's main strengths are plant anchoring, full-field preservation, receiver-flux risk visibility, multiple numerical gates, and reproducible packaging. Its main vulnerability is that it is not a full annual bankable redesign and does not yet have full-field custom-aimpoint/cross-code annual validation.

Therefore *Solar Energy* is high but defensible. It is a better first target than immediately downgrading because it is the cleanest audience match and has a no-mandatory-publication-fee subscription route. If rejected for insufficient validation depth, the prepared fallback should be *Sustainable Energy Technologies and Assessments* or *International Journal of Green Energy*, not a random low-rank solar journal.
